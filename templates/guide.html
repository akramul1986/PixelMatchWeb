📘 UI Testing Guide for New Team Members

Welcome to the team! This guide will help you understand how to use our "Digital Ruler" to find UI bugs quickly and accurately.

1. The "Digital Ruler" (Auto-Sync) Scaling

The Concept: High-resolution phones make everything look 3 times bigger than the original design. If we compare them as they are, the measurements will be wrong.

HOW WE CALCULATE SCALE:
If the Figma Design is 375 pixels wide, but your Phone Screenshot is 1125 pixels wide:
Calculation: 375 ÷ 1125 = 0.33
Result: The tool "shrinks" the app by 0.33x so it perfectly matches the Figma design.

Your Action: Always upload images and click "Match Figma Size" first to calibrate your ruler.

2. Measuring Elements Properties

When you click an element (like a button or icon), the tool finds the "Real World" size and translates it into the "Design Size" you need.

HOW WE CALCULATE SIZE:
Imagine you click a button that is physically 154 pixels on your screen. If your Scale is 0.95x:
Calculation: 154 (Screen Size) × 0.95 (Scale) = 146.3 pixels
Result: The tool shows 146px. If Figma says 150px, the developer made it 4px too small!

3. Padding & Spacing Internal Space

Padding is the "breathing room" inside an element (the gap between the text and the button's edge).

Example:
If the text "Submit" is touching the edges of the button, the padding is Zero (This is a bug!).
Standard Figma designs usually have 12px or 16px of padding to keep things looking clean.

Tester Tip: If the green lines don't automatically find the edge, grab the nicks (handles) and drag them to fit. The padding numbers will update instantly.

4. The "Ghost" Method Transparency

Use the Opacity Slider set to 50%. This puts the App Build on top of the Figma Design like a ghost.

  • Good: The text and icons overlap perfectly.
  • Bad: You see "double vision" (two icons next to each other). This means the developer put the icon in the wrong position.

5. How does Edge Sensitivity work? Transparency

Think of Edge Sensitivity as a "Color Contrast Filter." It tells the tool how much the color must change before it decides "this is the end of the button."

  • Low Sensitivity (like your 12): The tool is very "lax." It ignores subtle color changes (like soft shadows or glow effects) and looks for very strong, high-contrast borders.
  • High Sensitivity (40+): The tool is very "strict." Even a tiny change in the blue shade (like a gradient or a button shadow) will cause the tool to stop and draw a line there.

Real-World Example:

  • If you have a flat white button on a black background: You can use Low Sensitivity because the "edge" is very obvious.
  • If you have a light blue button on a dark blue background: You need High Sensitivity so the tool can "see" the slight difference between the two blues.

🚀 Step-by-Step Testing Checklist

  • Upload: Put your Figma design and App screenshot into the boxes.
  • Crop: Use the cropper to remove the phone's clock and battery bar.
  • Sync: Click Match Figma Size to calibrate the math.
  • Ghost Check: Slide Opacity to 50%. Check for "double vision" alignment issues.
  • Measure: Click 5-10 main buttons/images. If the Width or Height is even 1 pixel different from Figma, report it!
  • Red Proof: Look at the Red Proof (Diff Image). Large red areas show where the developer missed something entirely.
